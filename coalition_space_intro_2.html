01

What Is Coalition Space

Coalition Space is not for the unprepared. It is what happens when human beings push far enough out into the black that the nearest Union administrative office is a three-week transit away and the nearest Union military response is three weeks after that.

Geographically, Coalition Space encompasses the outer settlement clusters, ore belts, and transit corridors that form the trailing edge of organized human expansion in this region. The anchor is the Karrow Belt — a vast arc of mineral-rich debris that has driven settlement, commerce, and conflict here for over a century. Surrounding it are the shipping lanes, the deep-anchor stations, the agricultural settlements, and the long, dark stretches of nothing in between.

The people of Coalition Space tend to be practical, self-reliant, and possessed of very little patience for bureaucracy, except their own, which they have developed over generations.

Coalition Space sits at the intersection of frontier pragmatism and genuine civic pride. It is not a lawless frontier. It has laws, institutions, and culture. What it lacks is the dense administrative infrastructure of core Union space.

02

The Coalition

The Coalition began as a transit and trade compact between the six largest station-operators in the region, roughly one hundred and ninety years ago, when it became clear that Union administrative response times were incompatible with the pace of events in the Outer Bands.

The Union recognizes the Coalition as a semi-autonomous administrative authority; technically a local arm of Union governance, entitled to exercise Union administrative powers within defined parameters. In practice, the Coalition interprets those parameters generously.

The relationship is functional but permanently tense. Coalition space produces resources that Union wants. Union provides legitimacy and backstop guarantees that Coalition needs. Neither side entirely trusts the other.

03

Life in the Outer Bands

Most people in Coalition Space have never seen a mech outside of a construction or industrial context. The Lancer program exists here, but it is not glamorous in the way it might be in higher-profile theaters. There is, instead, the persistent and unspectacular work of keeping human civilization alive at the edge.

The Belt is the economic engine. Ore, ice, rare earth compounds, and trace elements fuel the refineries and the refineries fuel the settlements and the settlements are where people live and raise children and argue about water rights and run shops and fall in love and die of old age if they're lucky. This is the work of The Coalition.

Piracy is a real and persistent problem in the outer transit corridors. Claim-jumping in the Belt happens with enough regularity that the Guild has its own internal arbitration process. Corporate interests from the inner lanes periodically attempt to establish more formal footholds in Coalition Space, which the Coalition resists with the particular stubbornness of people who have built something themselves and do not want it taken from them by people with better lawyers.

The deep-anchor stations are the social hubs. Kessel-7, Port Amaranth, the Drift, Waystation Orvane. These are the places where routes intersect, where information moves, where deals are made and disputes are settled and people from very different lives end up at adjacent barstools. The culture of these stations is cosmopolitan.

"A falling knife has no handle."

04

Lancers in Coalition Space

In core Union space, becoming a Lancer is many things: a career track, a status marker, a military credential, the end result of a formal training pipeline. Here, it is something both simpler and more complicated. There are no training academies. There is no dedicated Lancer corps with uniforms and institutional culture. There is an exam, a licensing board, and a question that the licensing board is trying to answer: is this person capable of operating a mech responsibly in conditions that will test them?

Licensed Lancers in Coalition Space operate in a variety of roles: CDF contract work, private security for operators and transit companies, independent contracts, and occasionally direct engagement in crisis situations that don't fit neatly into any category. There is no mandatory service. There is, however, a cultural expectation that the license means something, and that using it purely for profit while the community that granted it suffers is a kind of failure.

This expectation is unwritten and unenforceable. It is also, in practice, nearly universal. Coalition Space is the kind of place where people remember who showed up and who didn't.

Lancers here are not heroes in the capital-H sense. They are highly capable people in a community that needs capable people and has found a way to certify them.

05

The Examination

The Coalition Lancer Licensing Examination is not a written test. It is not a simulation. The Licensing Board's position, held without exception since the board's founding, is that there is no substitute for finding out what a person does when the situation is real and the stakes are real.

Candidates are assigned an Everest-class general-frame mech chosen for its reliability, its versatility, and its lack of forgiveness for poor fundamentals. The Everest does not compensate for bad habits. It does not have systems that cover for operator error. What it does, it does solidly, and what you bring to it is what you have.

Each candidate receives an examination mission: a real operational situation that the CDF or a licensed Board contractor has flagged as appropriate for evaluation. The Board does not send candidates to die. It sends them somewhere they might, and watches what they do about it.

What the Board is looking for is not success in the narrow tactical sense — it is looking for judgment. Technical competence is the floor, not the ceiling.

The exam does not need to be a combat mission. It can be. It doesn't have to be. The Board licenses people who can fight; it also licenses people who can think, decide, and act under pressure.

Examination Structure

01
Assignment & Briefing

Candidates receive a dossier, a frame assignment, and a mission brief. The brief is accurate as of when it was written. Conditions in the field may have changed.

02
Deployment — LL0 / Everest Frame

Candidates deploy to the operational area with full access to their Coalition COMP/CON license at LL0.

03
The Mission

The candidate operates. There is no time limit explicitly stated.

04
Debrief & Certification

Candidates who complete the examination mission are certified at LL1. There is no ceremony.

You begin the campaign as examination candidates. You are not rookies. You are people who have been doing something difficult and real for long enough that the Board noticed you.

06

Getting Started — Who Are You?

Characters from Coalition Space tend to have histories. They were doing something before the exam. Most of that something was unglamorous and often dangerous and mattered in ways that didn't get written down anywhere.

Sample Backgrounds

Background What You Know What You're Learning
Belt Worker How the ore-field thinks. Close-quarters problem solving. Practical knowledge from dangerous systems. That some problems require a different kind of leverage than the ones you're used to.
CDF Veteran Small-unit tactics, Coalition military culture, the texture of institutional violence and its aftermath. That a license changes what you're authorized to do, but not what you're willing to.
Independent Hauler The lanes. Navigation. Risk calculus. How to read a situation from its margins. That you've been making high-stakes decisions alone. A frame just makes those decisions more expensive.
Star Rat What people look like when they've been waiting to die. Emergency systems, structural assessment, the geography of crisis. That a mech can go places a hauler can't, and reach problems before they become the kind you respond to after.
Station-Born How communities work, how information moves, where the pressure points are. The informal economy. That the station looking up at a mech in the sky is still the station that gave you everything you know.

"The Board doesn't want to know if you're brave. Everyone who walks in here is brave. They want to know if you're gonna crack under pressure."

— Attributed to a Licensing Board officer, exact source unknown

Coalition Space is a community at the edge of what it can manage. The dark is very large and the ships are very small and there are, right now, people in distress in a hundred places that no formal institution will reach in time.

You are going to be one of the people who can do something about that. Not the only thing. Not always the right thing. But something, when it matters, with your hands and your judgment and the machine they're about to trust you with.

That's enough.
